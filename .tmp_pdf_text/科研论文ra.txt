 A B S T R A C T
With the rapid growth of multimedia data on social networks, efficient and accurate cross-modal retrieval has 
become essential. Cross-modal hashing methods offer advantages such as fast retrieval speed and low storage 
cost. However, unsupervised deep cross-modal hashing methods often struggle with semantic misalignment and 
noise, limiting their effectiveness in capturing fine-grained relationships across modalities. To address these 
challenges, we propose Radial Adaptive Node Embedding Hashing (RANEH), designed to enhance semantic 
consistency and retrieval efficiency. Specifically, the semantic meta-similarity construction module reconstructs 
identity semantics using a similarity matrix, ensuring that hash codes retain modality-specific features. The 
radial adaptive hybrid coding method employs FastKAN as an encoder to map features into a shared hash 
space, maintaining semantic consistency across modalities. Lastly, the broadcasting node embedding unit 
leverages the Fast Kolmogorov–Arnold network to capture deep modality relationships, improving semantic 
alignment and node embedding accuracy. Experiments on the NUS-WIDE, MIRFlickr, and MSCOCO datasets 
show that RANEH method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art unsupervised cross-modal hashing methods 
in accuracy and efficiency. The codes are available at https://github.com/YunfeiChenMY/RANEH.
1. Introduction
With the rapid development of multimodal data, achieving ac-
curate cross-modal retrieval in large-scale heterogeneous data [1–4] 
has become a critical research challenge. Traditional unimodal re-
trieval methods fail to capture relationships between heterogeneous 
modalities, leading to weak semantic alignment and limited retrieval 
performance [5]. Various cross-modal retrieval paradigms have been 
developed to bridge this gap, each aiming to model inter-modal rela-
tionships more effectively. A fundamental approach is representation 
learning, where methods project different modalities into a shared 
feature space to enhance semantic alignment. While deep learning 
techniques such as contrastive learning [6] and large-scale pretrain-
ing [7] have demonstrated remarkable performance, they often require 
extensive labeled data and high computational resources. Alternatively, 
graph-based retrieval [8,9] captures structured relationships between 
modalities but faces scalability challenges on large datasets. Recently, 
self-supervised methods [10] have gained traction by reducing depen-
dency on manual annotations, yet issues like false negative contam-
ination remain unsolved. Among these paradigms, cross-modal hash-
ing [11–13] has emerged as a highly efficient solution due to its ability 
to transform high-dimensional multimodal data into compact binary 
codes. By reducing storage and computational costs, hashing-based 
methods improve retrieval efficiency while maintaining competitive

Although unsupervised cross-modal hashing does not rely on labeled 
data, it still faces two significant challenges: (1) Insufficient sensitivity 
and robustness to data quality. Unsupervised methods are more sensitive 
to noise and missing information in the data, which can lead to a 
lack of stability in the generated hash code. In addition, it is more 
challenging to maintain semantic similarity and relative relationships 
between data points in mapping data into binary hash codes, which 
directly affects the consistency of retrieval accuracy and effectiveness, 
especially when dealing with complex multimodal data. Several meth-
ods have been proposed to enhance the robustness of the model. For 
example, Hypergraph-Enhanced Hashing (HEH) [23], which optimizes 
the similarity matrix by leveraging frequency-based similarity calcula-
tions, and Fast Unsupervised Cross-Modal Hashing (FUCH) [24], which 
employs semantic-aware collective matrix factorization to improve fea-
ture discrimination and mitigate outliers. However, these methods 
rely on handcrafted similarity metrics or static latent representations. 
For instance, HEH’s frequency-based approach assumes uniform data 
distributions, making it less effective in noisy or imbalanced scenarios. 
(2) Insufficient cross-modal interactions and high computational complexity. 
The heterogeneity of different modalities makes it challenging to align 
and interact in a shared semantic space while preserving informa-
tion integrity. Existing methods [25,26] often utilize Graph Convo-
lutional Networks to enhance cross-modal interactions and maintain 
strong associative relationships. However, these approaches suffer from 
quadratic complexity when modeling pairwise relationships. Even ad-
vanced techniques like CMHML [19] require iterative label prediction, 
limiting scalability for large datasets. Thus, optimizing computational 
efficiency while improving performance remains a key challenge.
To address these challenges, this paper proposes a novel unsu-
pervised cross-modal hashing retrieval method, Radial Adaptive Node 
Embedding Hashing (RANEH). To improve sensitivity and robustness 
to data quality, the RANEH method ensures that the generated hash 
codes are aligned with the input modality features, effectively capturing 
meaningful identity semantics while mitigating the impact of noise 
or incomplete data, thereby enhancing retrieval performance. Further-
more, to overcome high computational complexity, the RANEH method 
employs FastKAN as an encoder, integrating adaptability with a radial 
structure to facilitate efficient feature learning, ensuring semantic con-
sistency across modalities while preserving the identity semantics of the 
data. Finally, by fully leveraging the Fast Kolmogorov–Arnold Network 
(FKAN), the RANEH method dynamically adjusts data distributions, op-
timizes cross-modal alignment, and further strengthens the interaction 
of cross-modal semantic features. The main contributions of this paper 
are as follows.
∙ The semantic similarity construction method is optimized to 
reconstruct identity semantics using a similarity matrix, effec-
tively preserving modality-specific features in hash codes and 
enhancing cross-modal retrieval accuracy.
∙ The radial adaptive hybrid coding method is proposed to map 
features from different modalities into a shared hash space, 
ensuring semantic consistency and preserving identity seman-
tics while mitigating cross-modal heterogeneity and optimizing 
computational complexity.
∙ The Fast Kolmogorov–Arnold network captures deep modality 
relationships and enhances semantic alignment, generating ro-
bust embeddings that further preserve identity semantics across 
modalities.
The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews recent advance-
ments in supervised and unsupervised cross-modal hashing methods. 
Section 3 details the RANEH method, including its framework and 
component analysis. Section 4 presents experimental evaluations of the 
RANEH method, comparing it with baseline methods and providing 
ablation experiments, convergence analysis, computational cost analy-
sis, t-SNE visualization and case study to demonstrate its effectiveness. 
Finally, Section 5 summarizes the contributions and suggests directions 
for future research.
2. Related work
Cross-modal hashing methods are mainly categorized into super-
vised and unsupervised cross-modal retrieval methods.
2.1. Supervised cross-modal hashing
Supervised cross-modal hashing [27–29] methods develop models 
to oversee the generation of binary hash codes that maintain intra- 
and inter-modal semantic consistency by exploiting explicit labeling 
information or similarity relations between pairs of samples. It ensures 
that samples from different modalities but with the same semantics 
produce similar hash codes. Individual mapping and asymmetric dual 
supervision for discrete cross-modal cleansing (IMADS) [30] combines 
modality-specific information with shared information, utilizing an 
asymmetric dual supervision framework to generate discriminative 
hash codes and reduce information loss through discrete optimization. 
Supervised Contrastive Discrete Hashing (SCDH) [31] extends unsu-
pervised contrastive methods to a supervised setting, leveraging class

labels to select positive and negative samples and directly generating 
discrete hash codes, enhancing feature representation and reducing 
quantization error. Efficient Supervised Graph Embedding Hashing (ES-
GEH) [32] effectively minimizes quantization errors by combining class 
label-based semantic embedding and graph embedding and avoids the 
explicit computation of the graph Laplacian through intermediate de-
composition. It introduces an iterative discrete optimization algorithm 
to enhance efficiency. Graph Convolutional Semi-Supervised Cross-
Modal Hashing (GCSCH) [33] reduces label dependence by generat-
ing pseudo-labels through consistency regularization, using a fusion 
network and GCN to capture semantic information, and applying a 
teacher–student learning scheme for knowledge transfer. Dual Semantic 
Fusion Hashing (DSFH) [34] explores cluster-level semantic relation-
ships in multi-label data by constructing fine-grained semantic simi-
larity, combines modality-specific representations with consensus hash 
codes, and fuses coarse-grained and fine-grained semantics to enhance 
hash code discrimination.
However, these methods still face key limitations. First, they rely 
heavily on accurate and large-scale labeled data, which limits their 
performance in noisy or imbalanced scenarios. Second, quantization 
errors remain a challenge that affects hash code quality. Third, most 
methods fail to capture the complex semantic relationships across 
modalities, hindering fine-grained alignment. Finally, the scalability 
of these approaches becomes an issue when handling large datasets. 
In conclusion, these supervised hashing methods [13,35] significantly 
enhance the accuracy and robustness of cross-modal hashing by incor-
porating label information. Experimental results demonstrate that these 
methods perform well on large-scale datasets and provide effective 
solutions for complex cross-modal retrieval tasks. Future research could 
explore ways to integrate multimodal information better and improve 
the generalization capability of hash codes without increasing compu-
tational complexity, addressing the growing demands of multimodal 
data.
2.2. Unsupervised cross-modal hashing
Unsupervised cross-modal hashing learns valid hash codes from 
unlabeled multimodal data by exploiting the intrinsic structure and 
similarity between modalities without relying on external labels. Many 
methods focus on similarity learning, utilizing graph structures and 
matrix decomposition to establish semantic associations across modal-
ities. Unsupervised Cross-Modal Hashing With Modality-Interaction 
(UCHM) [36] method enhances modality interaction, optimizes a hash-
similarity-friendly loss function to generate a high-quality similarity 
matrix, and introduces a bit-selection module during hashing net-
work training to produce high-quality binary codes. Unsupervised Dual 
Deep Hashing (UDDH) [37] method extracts deep features through a 
dual-deep hashing architecture and collaboratively encodes the binary 
codes with public semantic indices and modality content codes, effec-
tively bridging the semantic and heterogeneous gaps. Structure-Aware 
Contrastive Hashing (SACH) [38] combines high-dimensional and dis-
criminative representations learned by the network to construct a richer 
cross-modal semantic similarity matrix. It also designs a structure-
aware alignment network to reduce the disparity between hetero-
geneous data sources, thereby improving the performance of unsu-
pervised cross-modal retrieval. Unsupervised Contrastive Cross-modal 
Hashing (UCCH) [6] enables learnable hashing operations through a 
momentum optimizer, avoiding binary relaxation. At the same time, the 
proposed Cross-modal Ranking Learning Loss leverages a global strat-
egy to mitigate the impact of False Negative Pairs, improving retrieval 
performance. Hugs Bring Double Benefits: Unsupervised Cross-Modal 
Hashing with Multi-granularity Aligned Transformers (UCMH) [39] 
proposes a transformer-based multi-granularity learning framework 
that bridges modality gaps by capturing implicit semantics through 
fine-grained alignment and local embeddings. Furthermore, the pro-
posed differentiable optimization quantization and the extension of the 
hugging+ enhance retrieval performance and query results.
Existing unsupervised cross-modal hashing methods have signifi-
cantly enhanced cross-modal semantic alignment and retrieval per-
formance through techniques such as graph structures, matrix fac-
torization, and contrastive learning, demonstrating robust data pro-
cessing capabilities and innovative potential. However, unsupervised 
hashing still faces several challenges. Although recent methods [40,
41] capture semantic associations in multimodal data through matrix 
factorization, they exhibit certain limitations in modeling high-order 
semantic relationships. Additionally, [42] improves retrieval perfor-
mance by constructing multimodal fusion graphs, but their static graph 
structures may suffer from insufficient robustness under complex data 
distributions. To address these challenges, this paper enhances model 
robustness by optimizing the construction of the similarity matrix. 
Additionally, we propose an innovative encoder that effectively cap-
tures local features while preserving the identity semantics of different 
modalities. Meanwhile, FKAN is introduced to capture higher-order 
cross-modal joint semantic representations, addressing the limitations 
of existing methods in modeling high-order semantic relationships and 
ultimately improving retrieval performance.

3.2.3. Radial adaptive hybrid coding
FKAN’s exceptional ability to capture local features and reduce 
computational complexity enhances feature representation capabilities 
while improving overall efficiency, leading us to propose an innovative 
FKAN-based encoder. This encoder distinguishes itself from traditional 
MLP encoders by learning more complex nonlinear relationships. The 
RBF function calculates the Gaussian radial distances between the 
input data and these grid points, generating a set of basis function 
outputs. These basis functions capture the local features of the input 
data and are dynamically weighted through an adaptive SplineLinear 
combination, allowing them to adjust automatically for different inputs. 
This effectively forms a set of variable activation functions, overcoming 
the limitations of MLPs, which rely on fixed activation functions. 
Furthermore, the SiLU activation function is introduced to perform 
a stable and efficient transformation of the original input features. 
Replacing the traditional fully connected layer encoder with the FKAN-
based encoder significantly enhances the model’s expressiveness and 
generalization capabilities, making it more adaptable to complex data 
distributions. We input the original features 𝐗𝐼 and 𝐗𝑇 ) into FKAN to

4.7. Convergence analysis
In this section, we perform convergence analysis experiments on 
three datasets, MIRFlickr, NUS-WIDE, and MSCOCO, using 128-bit 
hash codes. The experimental results are shown in Fig.  5. On the 
MIRFlickr dataset, the model achieves stable convergence after 120 
cycles, and the loss value shows a smooth decreasing trend; on the NUS-
WIDE dataset, the model achieves convergence after 70 training cycles 
and shows a faster performance improvement; and on the MSCOCO 
dataset, the model achieves convergence in 50 cycles. As training 
iterations increase, the model’s performance gradually stabilizes across 
all datasets. Compared to existing methods, the RANEH method ex-
hibits significantly faster convergence on the MSCOCO dataset than 
UCCH [6]. The RANEH method achieves stable convergence within 50 
epochs, whereas UCCH requires approximately 80 epochs to converge. 
This indicates that the RANEH method can optimize model parameters 
more efficiently and reach convergence in fewer training iterations. 
Furthermore, compared to RSRH [22], our method exhibits a smoother 
convergence process, with the loss value decreasing more stably during 
training, thereby avoiding drastic fluctuations. This indicates that the 
RANEH method possesses greater robustness and stability in the op-
timization process. These results fully validate the effectiveness of the 
RANEH method in cross-modal retrieval tasks, as it not only accelerates 
model convergence but also enhances the stability of retrieval per-
formance, providing strong support for efficient cross-modal hashing 
learning.

4.9. t-SNE visualization
In this section, to intuitively demonstrate the performance of our 
proposed RANEH method in cross-modal hash retrieval tasks, we em-
ploy t-SNE [57] to visualize the discrete hash codes generated by the 
trained text and image hash networks on the MSCOCO dataset. Fig.  6 
illustrates the distribution of hash codes at different hash code lengths 
(16 bits, 32 bits, 64 bits, and 128 bits), where image hash codes and 
text hash codes are marked in red and blue, respectively.
The visualization results show that the clustering effect becomes 
more compact as the hash code length increases. When using 16-
bit hash codes, the hash representations of images and texts exhibit 
significant distribution overlap, and the boundaries between different 
categories remain relatively vague. However, when the hash code 
length increases to 64 bits or more, samples of the same category 
become more tightly clustered in the hash space. In contrast, the 
distinction between different categories becomes clearer. This trend 
indicates that more extended hash codes provide greater representa-
tional capacity, allowing the model to capture cross-modal semantic 
information accurately. This improvement can be attributed to two rea-
sons: (1) Increased information capacity. Longer hash codes can encode 
more feature information, enabling similar samples to be mapped to 
closer regions in the hash space, thereby improving clustering perfor-
mance. (2) Enhanced discriminability. When hash codes are short, the 
hash representations of different categories are more likely to collide, 
and samples from different categories may be mapped to the same 
hash value. In contrast, longer hash codes offer stronger discrimina-
tive power, ensuring better separation between different categories. 
In summary, experimental results demonstrate that appropriately in-
creasing the hash code length can effectively improve the performance 
of cross-modal hash retrieval. However, balancing storage costs and 
computational efficiency is crucial when selecting the optimal hash 
code length. Nevertheless, merely increasing the hash code length does 
not fully address the challenge of modality discrepancy in cross-modal 
retrieval. The key lies in effectively aligning feature representations 
across different modalities in the hash space.

generalization across different retrieval tasks, significantly improving 
retrieval accuracy in both T → I and I → T tasks. However, variations

(2) Applying data augmentation techniques to mitigate the effects of 
uneven data distribution and improve model generalization. By ad-
dressing these challenges, the RANEH method can be further refined 
to achieve higher robustness and accuracy, making it more effective 
for real-world cross-modal retrieval applications.
5. Conclusion
This paper proposes a novel Radial Adaptive Node Embedding 
Hashing (RANEH) method to address the challenge of maintaining se-
mantic consistency across modalities while capturing modality-specific 
features effectively. RANEH introduces three core innovations: Se-
mantic meta-similarity construction module, which ensures accurate 
semantic alignment by reconstructing identity semantics. Radial adap-
tive hybrid coding method that leverages FastKAN for efficient feature 
mapping into a shared hash space, preserving semantic consistency 
across modalities. Broadcasting node embedding unit that enhances 
feature representation by embedding cross-modal node relationships. 
These innovations significantly improve semantic alignment, maintain 
identity consistency, and enhance the efficiency and accuracy of hash 
learning. Extensive experiments demonstrate RANEH’s superior perfor-
mance in generating precise and effective hash codes for cross-modal 
retrieval tasks, highlighting its robustness and effectiveness in real-
world retrieval scenarios. By effectively bridging the semantic gap 
between modalities, the RANEH method offers promising applications 
in content-based retrieval, multimedia search, and cross-modal data 
mining.
